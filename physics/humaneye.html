1. The Human Eye

Structure and Parts

The human eye is one of the most valuable sense organs, allowing us to see the colourful world around us. It works like a natural camera.

[attachment_0](attachment)
  • Cornea: Thin membrane through which light enters. It acts as the primary refractive surface.
  • Iris: Dark muscular diaphragm that controls the size of the pupil.
  • Pupil: Controls the amount of light entering the eye. In bright light, the Iris contracts the pupil; in dim light, it expands the pupil.
  • Crystalline Lens: A convex lens (Converging lens) that provides the finer adjustment of focal length needed to focus the image exactly on the retina.
  • Ciliary Muscles: Control the curvature (and thus the focal length) of the eye lens.
  • Retina: The light-sensitive screen at the back of the eye. It contains Rods (for intensity/dim light) and Cones (for colour/bright light).
  • Optic Nerve: Transmits visual information (electrical signals) from the Retina to the Brain.
Power of Accommodation

It is the ability of the eye lens to adjust its focal length. This adjustment is achieved by the **Ciliary Muscles**:

  • **For Distant Objects:** Ciliary muscles relax, making the lens thin. The focal length **increases**.
  • **For Near Objects:** Ciliary muscles contract, making the lens thick (more curved). The focal length **decreases**.

Key Points:

  • Near Point (N): The minimum distance at which the eye can see an object clearly without strain. For a normal adult eye, this is **25 cm**.
  • Far Point (F): The maximum distance the eye can see clearly. For a normal eye, this is **Infinity ($\infty$)**.

2. Defects of Vision

Myopia (Nearsightedness)

Definition: A person with Myopia can see **near objects clearly** but **distant objects indistinctly**.

Causes:
  • Excessive curvature of the eye lens (becomes too thick).
  • Elongation of the eyeball (eye length increases).
Correction:

The image of a distant object is formed **in front of the retina**.

It is corrected by using a **Concave Lens** (Diverging lens) of appropriate power.

[attachment_1](attachment)
Hypermetropia (Farsightedness)

Definition: A person with Hypermetropia can see **distant objects clearly** but **near objects indistinctly**.

Causes:
  • Focal length of the eye lens is too long (lens is too thin).
  • Shortening of the eyeball (eye length decreases).
Correction:

The image of a near object is formed **behind the retina**.

It is corrected by using a **Convex Lens** (Converging lens) of appropriate power.

Presbyopia (Old Age Defect)

This defect arises due to the gradual weakening of the ciliary muscles and decreasing flexibility of the eye lens with age.

Symptoms & Correction:
  • The person finds it difficult to see near objects comfortably.
  • It is corrected by using a **Convex Lens**.
  • Sometimes, a person suffers from both Myopia and Hypermetropia; this is corrected using **Bifocal Lenses** (Concave lens on top, Convex lens on bottom).

3. Refraction, Dispersion, and Prism

Refraction through a Glass Prism

In a prism, the emergent ray is not parallel to the incident ray. The emergent ray deviates from the direction of the incident ray by an angle called the **Angle of Deviation ($\delta$)**.

The angle of deviation depends on the refractive index of the material and the angle of the prism.

Dispersion and Spectrum (VIBGYOR)

Dispersion: The splitting of white light into its constituent colours (the spectrum) when it passes through a transparent medium like a glass prism.

[attachment_2](attachment)
The Spectrum:
  • The sequence of colours is **VIBGYOR** (Violet, Indigo, Blue, Green, Yellow, Orange, Red).
  • **Violet** bends the **most** (shortest wavelength).
  • **Red** bends the **least** (longest wavelength).
Recombination (Newton's Experiment):

When the seven colours from the first prism are passed through a second, inverted prism, they recombine to give white light again.

Formation of a Rainbow

A rainbow is a natural spectrum appearing in the sky after a rain shower. It is caused by a combination of three phenomena occurring in water droplets:

  • **Refraction** and **Dispersion** of sunlight as it enters the droplet.
  • **Total Internal Reflection** inside the droplet.
  • **Refraction** as the light exits the droplet.

4. Atmospheric Effects

Atmospheric Refraction & Twinkling

Atmospheric Refraction: Caused by the continuous change in the refractive index of Earth's atmosphere (denser near ground, rarer higher up).

Twinkling of Stars:
  • The light from the stars undergoes repeated refraction as it passes through layers of air with constantly changing refractive indices (due to temperature/density changes).
  • This makes the apparent position of the star fluctuate rapidly, which is seen as **twinkling**.
  • **Planets do not twinkle** because they are closer and appear larger; they act as a collection of point sources, and the random variations cancel out.
Advanced Sunrise and Delayed Sunset:

Atmospheric refraction causes the Sun to be visible to us about **2 minutes before** the actual sunrise and for about **2 minutes after** the actual sunset.

Scattering of Light

Tyndall Effect: The scattering of light by colloidal particles or very fine suspended particles in a medium. (e.g., light path visible in a smoky room or forest canopy).

Why is the Sky Blue? (Rayleigh's Law)
  • The atmosphere has tiny air molecules (smaller than the wavelength of visible light).
  • Blue light (shorter wavelength) is scattered much **more strongly** than red light (longer wavelength).
  • When sunlight passes through the atmosphere, the blue colour is scattered all around the sky, making it appear blue.
Colour of Sun at Sunrise/Sunset:

At sunrise/sunset, the light travels the **longest distance** through the atmosphere.

  • Most of the short-wavelength (blue) light is scattered away from the line of sight.
  • The light that reaches our eyes is predominantly long-wavelength (red and orange) light. Hence, the Sun appears reddish.
